SN2 reaction

The antibond of a carbon-leaving group bond is typically low in energy and constitutes the LUMO for many molecules containing leaving groups. Note that the LUMO of the starting material in the reaction shown consists primarily of the C-Br antibond. However, in this molecule adjacent C-H bonds can align and hyperconjugate with the C-Br antibond. Consequently, the LUMO is extended to the hydrogens that are anti to the C-Br bond. When methoxide approaches (see the HOMO), it can attack the C-Br antibond (see the competing SN2 reaction) or one of the two hydrogens involved in hyperconjugation, as shown in the LUMO. Attack of a hydrogen results in the elimination of bromide ion, yielding an alkene. A major contributing factor to the reaction outcome in this case is the steric environment of the C-Br antibond. In the space-filling model, note that approach of methoxide to the C-Br antibond is hindered by the anti hydrogens. This steric hindrance will favor attack at one of the anti hydrogens rather than at the C-Br antibond. Products in this reaction result from both E2 and SN2 pathways (see the textbook). Remember that in reactions with a strong base/nucleophile such as methoxide, molecules containing primary leaving groups will give almost exclusively SN2 products, while those containing tertiary leaving groups will give E2 products.
